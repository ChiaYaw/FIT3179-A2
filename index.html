Victimisation in Australia from 2008 to 2022

Victimisation refers to the process of being made a victim of a crime or unfair treatment. It involves individuals experiencing harm, suffering, or loss due to the actions of others. Victimisation can occur in various forms, including physical assault, theft, and emotional abuse. Understanding victimisation is crucial for developing effective prevention strategies and providing support to those affected.

Physical Assault: A person is physically attacked or harmed.

Direct Assault: A person is threatened with harm in a face-to-face encounter.

Indirect Assault: Threats of harm made without a face-to-face interaction, such as through phone calls, messages, or other indirect means.

Combined Threats: Both face-to-face and non face-to-face threatened assaults. If a person has experienced both types, they are counted separately for each but only once in the total

Combined Assaults: All instances of physical assault and threatened assault. If a person has experienced both physical and threatened assault, they are counted separately for each type but only once in the total

Victimisation Rate by Sex and Age Group

From 2008 to 2022, physical assault victimisation rates in Australia have generally declined across all age groups and sexes. Males, especially those aged 15-29, consistently had higher rates compared to females. The overall downward trend suggests improvements in public safety measures or changes in reporting practices over the years. This decline highlights the effectiveness of targeted interventions and increased awareness about personal safety.

From 2008 to 2022, sexual assault victimisation rates for females in Australia varied across age groups. The 18-29 years age group saw fluctuations, with a notable increase from 1.3 in 2008 to 3.8 in 2021, before dropping to 2.3 in 2022. For females aged 30-44 years, the rates remained relatively low, peaking at 0.8 in 2016 and 2022. The 45 years and over age group consistently had the lowest rates, generally staying below 0.3 throughout the period.

Victimisation Rate by Household Crime

From 2008 to 2022, household crime victimisation rates in Australia generally declined across various crime types. Break-ins decreased from 3.3% in 2008 to 1.8% in 2022, while attempted break-ins fell from 3.1% to 1.9%. Motor vehicle theft rates dropped from 1.1% to 0.5%, and theft from motor vehicles decreased from 4.5% to 2.2%. Malicious property damage saw a significant reduction from 11.1% to 3.7%, and other thefts declined from 4.4% to 1.9%. These trends indicate a substantial improvement in household security and crime prevention measures over the years.

Victimisation Rate by State/Territory

New South Wales saw a decline from 2.8% in 2008 to 1.3% in 2022. Victoria’s rates also decreased from 3.3% to 1.6%. Queensland experienced fluctuations, ending at 2.3% in 2022. South Australia and Western Australia showed similar downward trends, with rates falling to 1.4% and 1.9%, respectively. Tasmania and the Northern Territory had higher initial rates, but both saw reductions, with Tasmania at 1.8% and the Northern Territory at 4.2% by 2022. The Australian Capital Territory’s rates decreased from 2.8% to 1.2%.
